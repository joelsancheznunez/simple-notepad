Immigration Processes & Legal Practices

Process Explanation & Examples
Employment-Based Immigration Visas for individuals seeking to work in the U.S. based on their professional skills, qualifications, or job offers.
Example: A software engineer receives an offer from a U.S. tech company and applies for an H-1B visa.
Family-Based Immigration Visas that allow U.S. citizens and lawful permanent residents to sponsor close family members for residency in the U.S.
Example: A U.S. citizen petitions for their spouse to immigrate to the U.S. and live as a permanent resident.
Naturalization The process through which a lawful permanent resident can become a U.S. citizen after meeting specific eligibility requirements.
Example: A green card holder applies for U.S. citizenship after five years of residency and passes the citizenship test.
Asylum Protection granted to individuals already in the U.S. who cannot return to their home country due to fear of persecution.
Example: An individual from a country with severe human rights violations applies for asylum to remain in the U.S.
Removal Defense Legal defense against deportation in immigration court, including appeals to the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA).
Example: A non-citizen facing deportation fights the case in immigration court with the help of a lawyer.
Criminal History Cases Immigration cases where the applicant’s criminal record is a significant factor in determining their eligibility for relief or status.
Example: An individual with a past criminal conviction seeks legal advice to determine how it impacts their immigration status.
Deportation The legal process of formally removing a non-citizen from the U.S. due to violations of immigration laws.
Example: An individual is deported after overstaying their visa and failing to obtain legal status.
Detentions The holding of individuals by immigration authorities while their immigration status is determined or pending removal.
Example: An individual is detained by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) while awaiting a hearing.
Refugees Individuals who seek protection and are granted the right to reside in the U.S. due to persecution in their home country.
Example: A family fleeing war in their home country is granted refugee status and allowed to settle in the U.S.
TPS (Temporary Protected Status) Grants temporary legal status to individuals from countries experiencing ongoing armed conflict, natural disasters, or other extraordinary conditions.
Example: An individual from a country hit by a severe earthquake is granted TPS and allowed to stay in the U.S. temporarily.
DACA (Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals) A program that protects eligible undocumented immigrants who came to the U.S. as children from deportation and provides work authorization.
Example: An individual who was brought to the U.S. as a child applies for DACA to avoid deportation and obtain a work permit.
VAWA (Violence Against Women Act) Provides immigration relief to victims of domestic violence who are abused by U.S. citizens or lawful permanent residents.
Example: A victim of domestic abuse self-petitions for legal status under VAWA without the abuser’s knowledge.
SIJS (Special Immigrant Juvenile Status) A pathway to legal residency for minors in the U.S. who have been abused, abandoned, or neglected by their parents.
Example: A minor who was abandoned by their parents applies for SIJS to obtain legal residency in the U.S.
U-Visas Visas for victims of certain crimes who cooperate with law enforcement in the investigation or prosecution.
Example: A victim of human trafficking assists law enforcement in the investigation and applies for a U-Visa.
T-Visas Visas for victims of human trafficking who cooperate with law enforcement in the investigation or prosecution.
Example: A victim of human trafficking receives a T-Visa after helping in the prosecution of the traffickers.
S-Visas Visas for individuals who assist U.S. law enforcement with investigations or prosecutions of criminal organizations.
Example: A key witness in a criminal organization case is granted an S-Visa for their cooperation.
Religious Visas Visas for religious workers to perform religious duties in the U.S.
Example: A pastor from another country receives a Religious Worker Visa to lead a congregation in the U.S.
Consular Processes The procedure of applying for a visa at a U.S. embassy or consulate outside the United States.
Example: An individual living abroad applies for a U.S. visa at the consulate in their home country.
